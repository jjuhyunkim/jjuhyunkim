      <h2>Personal Statement</h2>
      <p>
        DNA and RNA sequences underlie nearly all aspects of human biology, from molecular processes to complex
        traits. The interplay among DNA, RNA, and proteins forms a highly regulated system that governs cellular
        function. This complexity motivated my interest in bioinformatics and genomic research.
      </p>
      <p>
        During my PhD, I focused on GWAS to identify variants associated with phenotypes such as blood glucose and
        diabetes, with particular interest in interpreting intergenic loci. To improve interpretation, I integrated
        single-cell ATAC-seq data to evaluate regulatory variation in a cell type-specific context.
      </p>
      <p>
        I am particularly interested in telomere-to-telomere genome assemblies and difficult genomic regions that were
        missing or misassembled in earlier references. At NIH, I have contributed to multiple complete assembly
        projects and developed methods and tools for assembly curation, methylation interpretation, and structural
        variation analysis.
      </p>
    </section>
